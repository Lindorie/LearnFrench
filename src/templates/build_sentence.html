As you can see, there is a few differences between English and French.

Politeness tu/vous

The French language has 2 levels of politeness when it's about speaking to somebody. So the English you has 2 equivalents in French: tu (informal) and vous (formal). You have to know when to use one or the other one but it's a bit complicated: this graph could help...

Vous also means the plural form of tu, when the subject is a group of person.

On ≠ it

The other thing to note is the non equivalence between the English it and the French on. Indeed, in French, on means we in an informal situation. It also can be used to speak generally (general truth) or instead of "someone" (you don't know who) or "people" (in general):

"We" in an informal situation Instead of "someone"
In general, oral French use mainly on instead of nous. However, you shall always use nous in written formal French.
e.g. On est allés au cinéma hier soir. = Nous sommes allés au cinéma hier soir.		 You don't know who
e.g. On sonne à la porte. = Quelqu'un sonne à la porte.
Instead of "people" General truth
e.g. En France, on aime le fromage. = En France, les gens aiment le fromage. Mostly used in proverbs
e.g. Avec des si, on mettrait Paris en bouteille

Affirmative sentence

To build an affirmative sentence in French, you need to determine the subject and the verb.

Subject + Verb + complement(s)
e.g. Steven aime les brocolis.

Negative sentence

Basically, you have to add ne pas to the affirmative form.

Subject + ne + Verb + pas + complement(s)
e.g. Steven n'aime pas les brocolis.

When the verb starts by a vowel, write n' instead of ne to make a good liaison.

Question

To build a question sentence in French, take the affirmative form and switch the subject and the verb.

Verb + Subject + complement(s)
e.g. Aimes-tu les brocolis ?

Subject + Verb + Pronoun + complement(s)
e.g. Steven aime-t-il les brocolis ?

A more natural way to ask a question is to use Est-ce que ... ?

Est-ce que + affirmative form
e.g. Est-ce que Steven aime les brocolis ?
