Rules for pronunciation

The alphabet

The French alphabet, like in English, has 26 letters.

To know how to pronounce French words, you have to master the pronunciation of each letter of the alphabet. Let's begin!

A B C D E F
H I J K L M N
O P Q R S T U
V W X Y Z

Accents and cedilla

The French alphabet differs from English because there can be accents on certain letters or a mark like the cedilla.They are used to change the pronunciation or the meaning of the words.

Accents on the letter E

There are four accents: acute (é), grave (è), circumflex (ê) and trema (ë). They indicate different sounds:

é
e.g. cinéma		 è, ê, ë
e.g. crème, fête, Noël
Accents on the letters A, U, O

Accents on these letters can be used in writing to differentiate meaning, even when the pronunciation is the same:

a / à, la / là
Voilà		 ou / où, sur / sûr
Août, ambigü		 Ô capitaine, mon capitaine ! (Dead Poets Society), ôter
Cedilla

The cedilla, ç, is used before a, o, u and sounds the same as the letter S.

français leçon reçu

Other sounds

The French language has many other sounds that you have to know for the correct pronunciation.

Vowels

There are 6 vowels in French: a, e, i, o, u, y.

These vowels can be combined to create specifics sounds:

au, eau
e.g. autre, château		 ou
e.g. rouge		 ai
e.g. aimer		 oi
e.g. croissant		 eu, oe, oeu
e.g. bleu, oenologie, coeur

Nasal sounds

There is no equivalent in English for these sounds, so you have to try pronounce them in the right way and learn them by heart.

un
e.g. un		 on
e.g. bon		 in, ain, ein
e.g. vin, pain, rein		 an, en, em-
e.g blanc, vent, emploi/emmener

Be careful when you see these combinations in a word, because it could be a different pronunciation, depending on the place inside the word and which letter(s) there is (are) after.

If you listen to the following words, they differ on the pronunciation, mainly because there is a vowel directly after the nasal sound. So you have to split the word into different syllables.

For example:

une
u-ne		 bonne
bo-nne		 farine, vaine,
fa-ri-ne, vai-ne		 canne
ca-nne

The nasal sounds en and ein are specifics as you can't split the word into several syllables but it becomes a different sound. Basically, it's like you pronounce the letter N.

This sound is also shared by many different spellings:

renne, ennemi, tennis... peine, reine, veine... saine, vaine... rêne, pêne... Cène

Consonants

The French R sound is probably the hardest for English people. It comes from the throat.

adresse dormir ronronner

A specific sound can be created with a combination of (vowel) + IL (+LE).

-eille/-eil
e.g. bouteille/éveil		 -ouille/-ouil
e.g. grenouille/fenouil		 -aille/-ail
e.g. canaille/évantail		 -ille
e.g. jonquille		 -euille/-euil
e.g. feuille/chevreuil

The ch and j are a bit different from English, be careful of that. Indeed, the French ch is similar to English sh, as in "shot", and the French j is similar to English s in the word "Asia".

Also, the J and the G have the same pronunciation when G is followed by the letter E or I.
chanson, panache je, jouer, Juillet
genre, beige, algèbre
girafe, agile

When the letter G is followed by another letter, the sound is the same as the English g:

gare, bague, groupe, gorille

Silent letters

Unlike in English, the letter H in the beginning of a word is silent.

hôpital, hibou, héros, habiter

Sometimes you mustn't pronounce all the letters at the end of a word or inside a word:

restaurant, Paris, dessert, canard, doux longtemps

But sometimes you have to pronounce the final letter:

lac, chenil, mer

Native French speakers can sometimes drop the letter E inside a word:

p'tit (petit), sam(e)di (samedi)
